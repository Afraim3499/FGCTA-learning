# Lurnava Beginner Explanation Standard

## Purpose

Lurnava teaches learners from absolute zero. Assume the learner does not know trading, chart reading, market mechanics, or common trading vocabulary.

The goal is not to sound advanced. The goal is to make advanced thinking understandable through clear definitions, real situations, relevant visuals, and practical examples.

## Core Rule

Every sentence must have context.

A sentence must do at least one of these:

1. define a term
2. explain why it matters
3. show a real situation
4. connect to learner behavior
5. prevent a mistake
6. guide a decision
7. prepare the next idea

If a sentence does none of these, remove or rewrite it.

## Technical Term Rule

Whenever a technical term appears for the first time in a module, explain it immediately.

Use this structure:

1. Plain definition
2. Beginner analogy
3. Market example
4. What it does not mean

Example:

“Spread means the small gap between the price buyers are offering and the price sellers are asking. A simple analogy is a shop: the shop may sell an item for one price but buy it back for a slightly lower price. In markets, that gap is the spread. Spread does not tell you direction; it only tells you about transaction cost and market conditions.”

## Beginner Context Rule

Never drop a statement without a situation.

Bad:
“Do not rush practice.”

Better:
“You have 30 minutes before sleep, and the chart is moving quickly. The rushed learner clicks through five practice actions to feel productive. The trained learner chooses one concept, studies one example, writes one reason, and saves one review note.”

## Visual Requirement Rule

If the learner must picture something, show it.

Use visuals for:

- market objects
- chart elements
- patterns
- structures
- scenarios
- mechanisms
- comparisons
- workflows

Use the best visual type:

- coded diagram for anatomy or mechanism
- coded chart for structure or market behavior
- static image for realistic scenario reference
- comparison panel for wrong vs better
- timeline for sequence or sessions
- checklist for procedure

## Example Quality Rule

Examples must be:

- easy to imagine
- realistic
- directly connected to the lesson
- free from unnecessary jargon
- useful for a beginner

Avoid examples that require advanced market knowledge to understand.

## No Bookish Knowledge Rule

Do not write abstract education.

Bad:
“Market reading requires discipline and evidence.”

Better:
“You see a large candle and feel like something important is happening. Instead of reacting, you ask: what exactly changed? Did price break a structure point, or is this only one large candle inside the same range? That question turns emotion into observation.”

## Analogy Rule

Use analogies when they clarify a concept.

Good analogy types:

- driving school
- flight simulator
- medical diagnosis
- business records
- shop inventory
- sports training
- black-box recorder
- engineering inspection

Do not use analogies that are dramatic, unrealistic, or disconnected from the lesson.

## Roadway Context Rule

For every module, explain how the idea changes across:

1. Forex
2. Gold
3. Crypto

Each roadway must include:

- simple explanation
- roadway-specific scenario
- common beginner mistake
- visual or visual-ready structure
- mini drill or decision question

## Statement Ban

Avoid unsupported direct claims such as:

- “This is important.”
- “This is professional.”
- “This is high probability.”
- “This is how institutions trade.”
- “This will help you win.”
- “Always do this.”

Replace them with context, condition, or scenario.

## Safe Language Rule

Avoid:

- guaranteed
- profit promise
- exact entry
- buy/sell instruction
- signal service framing
- smart money/guru language
- manipulation language
- live trade instruction

Prefer:

- observe
- classify
- review
- evidence
- uncertainty
- simulation
- practice action
- journal note
- historical scenario
- roadway context

## Card Review Checklist

Before accepting a card, answer:

1. Can a beginner understand every term?
2. Is there a real situation or example?
3. Is there a visual when the idea needs one?
4. Does the card prevent a real mistake?
5. Does the learner know what to do with the idea?
6. Is this better than free generic content?
7. Does every sentence have a reason to exist?

If any answer is no, the card needs revision.
