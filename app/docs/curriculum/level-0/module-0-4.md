# Module 0.4: The Hierarchy of Evidence

**Objective**: Understand the weight and hierarchy of market data layers.

---

## 1. Core Curriculum

### 1.1 Mission Brief: The Evidence Hierarchy
**Label**: Core Foundation
**Visual Key**: `time-compression-board`
**Body**: A timeframe is not just a 'zoom level'; it is a filter for **Evidence Weight**. Each candle records the net commitment of participants over a fixed duration. A larger record (like a Daily candle) captures more capital and more strategic decisions than a smaller one (like a 1-minute candle).

#### Context Details:
*   **Key Terms**:
    *   **Evidence Weight**: The relative importance of a record based on the time and volume it represents.
    *   **Institutional Commitment**: The long-term market direction driven by large-scale participants.
    *   **Data Compression**: The process of summarizing smaller market events into a larger, more stable record.
*   **Why This Matters**: If you don't understand weight, you will treat a 1-minute 'wiggle' with the same importance as a Daily 'shift.' This is the primary cause of retail confusion.
*   **Real Life Example**: A Daily candle is a book; a 1-minute candle is a single sentence. You cannot understand the story by only reading the sentences in isolation.
*   **Common Mistake**: Treating all timeframes as equally important.
*   **Quick Note**: More time inside a candle equals more evidence weight.

### 1.2 Visual Intro: Narrative Layers
**Label**: Structural Mapping
**Visual Key**: `timeframe-comparison-windows`
**Body**: The market is a stack of stories (Narratives) happening simultaneously. A lower-timeframe 'story' might look urgent, but it is often just a small detail inside a much larger institutional narrative. A professional learner identifies which layer is currently in control.

#### Context Details:
*   **Key Terms**:
    *   **Narrative Layer**: The specific market story told by a selected timeframe.
    *   **The Detail**: Short-term behavior visible on lower timeframes.
    *   **The Narrative**: The broader, more stable direction visible on higher timeframes.
*   **Why This Matters**: To avoid traps, you must know if the current move is a 'New Narrative' or just 'Detail' within an old one.
*   **Real Life Example**: A city bus (LTF) might turn left to avoid a pothole, but the route (HTF) is still moving North.
*   **Common Mistake**: Thinking a local turn changes the global route.
*   **Quick Note**: The Narrative layer defines the environment for the Detail layer.

### 1.3 Visual Intro: The Weight of the Record
**Label**: Quantitative Logic
**Visual Key**: `timeframe-weight-ladder`
**Body**: Not all evidence is created equal. We use a **Weight Hierarchy** to prioritize our readings. Higher timeframes carry more 'Structural Gravity'—they are harder to move and represent a more significant consensus among participants.

#### Context Details:
*   **Key Terms**:
    *   **Structural Gravity**: The tendency of price to respect larger-timeframe levels over smaller ones.
    *   **Evidence Priority**: The rule of giving more weight to larger-scale records during an audit.
*   **Why This Matters**: A $750 student knows that 'Noise' (LTF) is frequent, but 'Narrative' (HTF) is reliable. We stack evidence from the top down.
*   **Real Life Example**: A daily rejection wick has 'Heavy Weight.' A 1-minute rejection wick has 'Light Weight.'
*   **Common Mistake**: Ignoring the gravity of the higher timeframe.
*   **Quick Note**: Higher timeframe = Higher priority.

### 1.4 Visual Intro: Institutional Signature vs. Retail Noise
**Label**: Participant Roles
**Visual Key**: `htf-ltf-roles`
**Body**: To read like a professional, you must distinguish between the **Signature** and the **Noise**.

* **Institutional Signature (HTF)**: Records the strategic commitment of banks and sovereign funds.
* **Retail Noise (LTF)**: Records tactical wiggles, algorithmic spikes, and emotional reactions.

#### Context Details:
*   **Key Terms**:
    *   **Institutional Signature**: Clear, high-weight evidence left by large-scale participants.
    *   **Retail Noise**: Lower-weight, unstable movement often driven by speculation and HFTs.
*   **Why This Matters**: This distinction protects you from 'chasing' noise and allows you to align with real capital flow.
*   **Real Life Example**: The 'Compass' (Signature) shows the destination; the 'Microscope' (Noise) shows the dust on the road.
*   **Common Mistake**: Chasing the noise and missing the signature.
*   **Quick Note**: Read the Signature before you study the Noise.

### 1.5 Visual Intro: Resolving Timeframe Conflict
**Label**: Evidence Auditing
**Visual Key**: `timeframe-conflict-board`
**Body**: Timeframes often 'disagree.' A lower-timeframe push can be active while a higher-timeframe wall is still holding. A disciplined learner does not guess who will win; they classify the evidence as **Mixed** and wait for alignment.

#### Context Details:
*   **Key Terms**:
    *   **Alignment**: When multiple timeframe layers support the same reading.
    *   **Mixed Evidence**: A condition where hierarchy layers are in conflict.
    *   **Wait for Consensus**: The professional act of staying 'Unclear' until alignment returns.
*   **Why This Matters**: Conflict is the leading cause of emotional trading. Labeling it as 'Mixed' removes the pressure to decide.
*   **Real Life Example**: A 5m rally (Noise) into a 4H resistance (Signature). The high-weight layer is still the boss.
*   **Common Mistake**: Forcing a 'Bullish' or 'Bearish' decision when the evidence is mixed.
*   **Quick Note**: If the layers disagree, the reading is 'Not Clear Yet.'

### 1.6 Visual Intro: The Evidence Stack Workflow
**Label**: Process Control
**Visual Key**: `top-down-reading-workflow`
**Body**: A clinical reading always moves from the **Outside In**. We audit the Narrative first, define the Structure second, and use the Detail only to refine the final execution. If the layers do not stack, the reading quality is low.

#### Context Details:
*   **Key Terms**:
    *   **Outside-In Audit**: Starting with the highest timeframe and moving down.
    *   **Evidence Stack**: A collection of facts from multiple layers that support one conclusion.
    *   **Reading Quality**: The strength of a conclusion based on its evidence alignment.
*   **Why This Matters**: Starting from the 'inside' (the smallest chart) causes you to hallucinate opportunities that the bigger narrative doesn't support.
*   **Real Life Example**: Checking the weather (HTF) before looking at the engine (LTF) of the plane.
*   **Common Mistake**: Starting on the 1-minute chart and then trying to find a reason to buy on the Daily.
*   **Quick Note**: Narrative first. Detail last.

### 1.7 Practice: Final Audit Practice
**Label**: Strategic Decision
**Visual Key**: `timeframe-decision-scenario`
**Body**: This scenario tests your ability to resolve conflict. You see an aggressive 5-minute push (Detail) moving directly into a major 4-hour resistance level (Narrative). What is the disciplined reading?

#### Task:
*   **Question**: What is the disciplined reading?
*   **Options**:
    *   **A**: The 5m push is very strong; the market has turned bullish.
    *   **B**: The 4H resistance is old; the 5m move is the current truth.
    *   **C**: The evidence is mixed. The 5m move is active, but the 4H Narrative is still bearish. I will stay 'Unclear' and wait for alignment. (Correct)
    *   **D**: I will buy a small amount just to see what happens.
*   **Feedback**:
    *   **A**: Incorrect. You are giving 'Detail' more weight than 'Narrative.' A 5m move rarely overrides 4H resistance without significant work.
    *   **B**: Incorrect. Newer does not mean more important. Institutional signatures on 4H carry more weight than 5m algorithmic noise.
    *   **C**: Correct. You have correctly classified the conflict. A professional stays patient when the 'Evidence Stack' is not aligned.
    *   **D**: Incorrect. This is 'Gambling,' not 'Reading.' We act on evidence alignment, not curiosity.
